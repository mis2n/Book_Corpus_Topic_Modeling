DEPARTMENT OF PUBLIC INSTRUCTION. 
NasHviLir, Trenn., December 1, 1905. 
To His Excellency, John I. Cox, Governor of Tennessee : 
In compliance with Section 7, Chapter 25, of the Acts of 1878, 
I herewith submit the Annual Report of the Department of 
Instruction for the year ending June 30, 1905. 
SraTisvics. 
A careful study of the statistics of the public school system 
of Tennessee from the time of the enactment of the present law 
in 1873 to the present is not only interesting, but instructive. 
Tennessee has built up a school system without help from any 
source, except the resources of its own people. Our public school 
fund is only a certificate of indebtedness signed by the Governor 
of the State, on which the State pays interest at the rate of 6 per 
cent per annum. The law establishing this system provided 
that the county could levy a tax in addition to the 15 cents on 
the $100 levied by the State. Within a few years after every 
county in the State complied with the law, and the increase in 
local taxation has been gradual from that day. Within the last 
few years the increase in local taxation has been very marked, 
as the figures of this report will show. For the past six years 
the increase in the scholastic population has been very little, 
while the enrollment in the schools for the year ending last June 
is 22,760 more than it was three years ago, and for the past year 
it was 5,093 more than the preceding year. The average daily 
attendance increased last year 3,806. Especial attention is called 
to the increase in the value of school property. Three years ago 
the school property of the State was valued at $4,179,123. This 
report shows $5,171,753, an increase of $992,630. Last year we 
spent for school buildings $254,872. This does not include a 
number of buildings erected on bonds issued by the large cities 
of the State. The average salary of teachers in rural districts 
has increased within three years from $28.96 to $34.87. The 
most noticeable improvement is in the total receipts from all

sources. In 1902 we received for public schools in the State 
$1,883,744, and for the year ending June 30, 1905, our receipts 
were $3,101,847. This increase includes the appropriation from 
the surplus fund January 1, 1905, the income from the increased 
valuation of property in the State and the increase in local taxa- 
tion. It will be noticed that as this report closes June 30, 1905, 
a large part of the surplus remained in the balance on hand and 
is being used for the schools during the current term. The in- 
crease in the average number of days taught in the State within 
three years is 18, or from 95 days to 113. I am satisfied that the 
current term will show over 120 days. Attention is also called to 
the fact that within the past year teachers’ institutes were held in 
nearly every county in the State and the number of teachers 
attending was perhaps larger than ever before. These figures 
give cause for encouragement and congratulation. They also 
demonstrate the fact that the additional help given by the 
schools on the part of the State has been met by a corresponding 
amount by the counties and municipalities, and it again empha- 
sizes the fact discussed in our last annual report, that the Ten- 
nessee system is established upon the idea that the education of 
the children of the State is jointly a responsibility of the State, 
county and city authorities. Isee no reason for any change of 
our present system of taxation, except that we should continue 
to increase appropriations from State and counties. Notwith- 
standing the great advance we have made in our public school 
revenues there is yet cause for considerable additions. The 
average per capita distribution for schools in the United States 
is a little over $11, while in Tennessee it is under $3. 
EpucaTioNAL CAMPAIGN. 
In April, 1903, a meeting of superintendents, directors and 
citizens from all parts of the State was held in the city of Nash- 
ville. It was decided to undertake then a thorough campaign 
of the State for better schools, more money, better buildings and 
better teachers. This campaign has been carried on in all parts 
of the State and educational gatherings have been held in every 
county. In this work we had the help of prominent men from 
all professions and the result is that there is a healthy sentiment 
for public schools, and in every part of the State people are de- 
manding better schools in every particular. They are beginning 
to see the need of better facilities, and the increase by counties

in rate of taxation shows that the people are willing to pay for 
the schools. 
In East Tennessee a campaign committee was organized and 
the campaign carried on under the direction of this committee. 
Last summer and fall educational gatherings were held in every 
county in East Tennessee under the direction of Prof. P. P. Clax- 
ton, the chairman of the East Tennessee committee. The State 
Superintendent attended and participated in nearly all of these. 
Every county in that section of the State was visited and meet- 
ings of the people held. These gatherings numbered from 600 
to 5,000 each, and the interest manifested indicated the desire of 
the people for better conditions. The campaign has been carried 
on in Middle and West Tennessee, under the direction of the 
State Superintendent, with like results. Where it has been 
possible educational speakers have appeared before the county 
courts, and have always been given attentive and earnest hearing. 
The Southern Education Board has given material help in 
this work by furnishing money to carry on the campaign This 
fund has been used under the direction of Prof. Claxton, and the 
work accomplished has shown the wisdom of the board in the 
appropriation and the manner of its use. Weare under lasting 
obligations to this board and to Prof. Claxton for the great work 
they have accomplished. 
It is the purpose of the department to continue the campaign 
through this year, and to especially urge the erection of good 
buildings in the rural districts, the employment of only compe- 
tent teachers and increased attendance. 
In the work of the campaign the press of the State has given 
most valuable help. They have contributed not only space for 
notices of meetings, but their columns have been filled with ed- 
itorials urging upon the people the improvements advocated in 
the campaign. The press of Tennessee has shown itself the 
warm advocate of popular education, and I believe has accom- 
plished as much as any other agency in bringing about the 
healthy condition that exists today. 
Rurat Scuoots. 
Of the 722,000 children enumerated in the State over 600,000 
live in the small towns and rural districts. The great problem 
of school administration then must be the improvement of the 
rural schools. The time has come when all recognize the fact

—20— 
that the farmer, above all others, needs a thorough and practical 
education. He is dealing and contending with all professions 
and avocations, and he comes in contact with every line of busi- 
ness. In his daily life he needs a knowledge of all the sciences, 
and in his relations with the commercial and industrial world 
he has a daily demand for practical understanding of those 
branches which underlie a knowledge of the great business world. 
The preparation of the farmer’s boy for the duties of life is the 
work of the rural school. These schools then should be made 
equal to, if not better, than any city system. The great need of 
more system in the management of our country schools is recog- 
nized by all who have had any dealings with their work. The 
_Telations of the primary, secondary and rural high school should 
be better established and their interdependence recognized. 
TEACHERS FOR THE RuRaL ScHOOLs. 
Our instructions to County Superintendents last May con- 
tained the following : 
“T cannot too strongly impress upon you the necessity for 
great care in the examination and licensing of teachers. Under 
no consideration should an incompetent teacher be employed to 
teach in our public schools. The State and counties are making 
increased appropriations for schools, and the average salary as 
well as the length of the school term in the State is considerably 
advanced. This should be met with corresponding improve- 
ment in the efficiency of the teachers, 
“ Examinations on all branches required to be taught in the 
public schools and upon theory and practice of teaching should 
be thorough, practical and uniform, and only persons possessing 
ability to teach and of good moral character should be granted 
certificates. You have no right to grant a certificate to a person 
who has failed as a teacher or who is of doubtful character. The 
fact that a person holds a certificate from a State institute does 
not justify you in issuing him a certificate if you are not satisfied 
as to his moral character. In brief, you are instructed to license 
only persons who are in every way worthy, capable and thor- 
oughly qualified. 
“You are especially instructed to refuse certificates to appli- 
cants who are deficient in spelling and the use of good English, 
even when the grades on other branches cause the average to be 
above that required.”

—21— 
We recognize the fact that it was practicaily impossible for 
County Superintendents to adhere rigidly to these instructions. 
This would have left at least 40 per cent of the schools of the 
State without teachers. I believe they did the very best they 
could, and the large number of persons refused certificates in the 
. State shows that these officials are trying to elevate the standard 
of teachers. One of the most serious problems now confronting 
the public schools of this State is the preparation of teachers. 
Tennessee should by all means have at least three good State 
normal schools, and appropriations for this purpose would be 
more than saved by avoiding the great waste of money now paid 
for incompetent teachers. Teaching has become a profession, 
and all students of education know that the untrained and 
unskilled teacher wastes much effort and time in teaching things 
for which the child is not prepared, and in not understanding 
how to present subjects suited to the young minds and especially 
adapted for the cultivation of the child’s faculties at different 
stages. 
It is poor economy to withhold from those who would pre- 
pare for the responsible work of teaching opportunities for such 
preparation. At present our rural teachers receive no profes- 
sional training except from teachers’ institutes and the summer 
schools conducted in the State. Weare demanding from them 
examination on the subjects required to be taught, but on ac- 
count of the absence of professional schools we are not able to 
demand that professional training which is necessary for our 
teachers. During the past few years many County Superintend- 
ents have licensed teachers as young as sixteen years on account 
of the scarcity of the teachers. No person under eighteen years 
should assume the responsibility of a teacher in the public 
schools. The sixteen-year-old girl may have a sufficient knowl- 
edge of the branches taught, but she does not possess the neces- 
sary tact and judgment to conduct a good school. Maturity of 
thought and judgment are necessary qualifications of a success- 
ful teacher. Another great evil has been the employment of 
teachers related to district directors. With many of these officials 
the greatest ambition seems to be to secure a place for some rela- 
tive. In all such cases the temptation for “a trade” is too great 
to be resisted and “a community of interests” is established at 
the expense of the schools, Legislation should be enacted that 
will prevent this.

It is not sufficient that teachers be prepared for their work 
when they.secure certificates, but that teacher who does not con- 
tinue to improve is poorly fitted to instruct and guide the young 
in securing an education and in the formation of character. 
Professional reading courses and attendance upon educational 
meetings should be made compulsory, and n> teacher, however * 
well qualified, should be exempt from these requirements. The 
Public School Officers’ Association at its January meeting 
authorized the appointment of a committee to arrange a reading 
course for the teachers of the State. I suggest that the Legisla- 
ture enact such laws as will render the act of this body more 
effective. 
Summer ScHoots anp Teacuers’ INsTITUTES. 
The great work accomplished by the Summer School of the 
South at Knoxville and the Peabody College for Teachers cannot 
be overestimated. While the attendance at these schools has 
been mostly from the class of teachers employed in the cities, 
towns and best schools of the State, the influence has extended 
through them to the lower schools, and the excellent effects have 
been felt by all. 
Teachers’ institutes were held last summer in all the counties 
of the State, and we estimate that at least 75 per cent of the 
teachers attended eithera summer school or a teachers’ institute. 
Efforts have been made to improve the efficiency of the insti- 
tutes, but to employ a sufficient number of competent institute 
instructors will require more money than the State now appro- 
priates. We held last summer eight State institutes for white 
teachers and four for colored. These were of one month dura- 
tion each. In addition to this the State furnished help to thirty- 
three counties in holding county institutes. 
A sufficient amount should be appropriated to enable the 
State Department to send at least one good instructor to every 
* county in the State. The great need at present is improvement 
in primary methods and the work of our institutes should deal 
largely with this subject. More training, knowledge, character 
and professional skill are required for teaching a primary school 
than for any other grade. 
CoNSOLIDATION AND TRANSPORTATION. 
The Act of 1903 making school and civil districts coextensive 
and providing that no school shall be maintained with under

70 scholastic population was intended to bring about a consoli- 
dation of weak schools over the State. This purpose was accom- 
plished to some extent, but there are yet too many of these small 
schools maintained. The little school does not have a sufficient 
income to employ a competent teacher and run a reasonable 
length of time. There is not sufficient opportunity for classi- 
fication and gradation of pupils. There is not enough competi- 
tion to arouse the best energies of the child. I believe the time 
has come when many counties in the State could safely establish 
central district schools and at public expense transfer pupils 
living at a great distance from theschool. This plan has worked 
well in a number of the Central and Eastern States, and I see no 
reason why those counties in Tennessee that have built good 
roads could not adopt it. 
Trecommend that the act of 1903 be so amended as to force

violation of that act. 
County HieH ScHoors. 
In 1899 the Legislature passed an act authorizing the county 
courts where they see proper to establish one or more county 
high schools. The law is in every particular a good one. The 
high schools are necessary to complete the county system. If 
established and properly managed they would do much towards 
furnishing competent teachers for the rural schools and the 
counties would receive benefits worth much more than the cost 
of maintenance. In this age the farmer’s boy needs a broader 
and more general education than can be secured in the primary 
and secondary schools. The curriculum of the high school 
should prepare for the practical duties of life and should make 
special provision for agricultural and industrial teaching. They 
should give an opportunity for the country boy to prepare for 
college and university without having to attend private schools. 
Within the past two years, through the efforts of Prof. Clax- 
ton and other educators, a number of these schools have been 
established, and it is to be hoped that before the close of the 
present year many more counties will provide for them.

County SUPERVISION. 
The act of 1895 requires all candidates for the office of County 
Superintendent to present a certificate of qualification from the 
State Board of Education. Examinations for these certificates are 
held in October preceding the biennial election. We have in Ten- 
nessee a body of efficient, loyal and earnest County Superintend- 
ents. Unfortunately the salary fixed by the County Court for this

office is, in many cases, too low, and a very large per cent of them

On this account they cannot give that careful attention to the

GEORGE D. HOLMES. 
George Duffield Holmes, who for nearly half a century held 
an important place in the education of the youths of West Ten- 
nessee, was born in North Mississippi, November 18, 1831. His 
parents, Rev. James Holmes, D. D., and wife, had come from New 
Jersey in 1826 as missionaries to the Indians in Mississippi. On 
the removal of the Indians in 1838 to the Territory, Dr. Holmes, 
being unable on account of his health to go with them, moved 
to Tennessee. At Mount Carmel, Tipton County, he, a gradu- 
ate of Princeton College, opened a boarding school for boys, 
preparing them for college. 
Thus was George D. Holmes from infancy surrounded by 
such influences—broad education and true consecration to God— 
as would tend to make a deep impression on mind and heart. 
Early in life Dr. Holmes united with the Presbyterian Church, 
and later became an elder, his chief object in life being to glorify 
God. 
Entering Princeton College at the age of 14, he graduated at 
18, and was at once offered a position in West Tennessee College 
—now the Baptist University—at Jackson, of which his father 
was then president. In 1857 he and his father were asked to 
take charge of the Tipton Female Seminary, Covington, Tenn. 
Here was Mr. Holmes’ great work. Here for forty years he de- 
voted his heart and brain to training the minds and developing 
the character of the young ladies under his care. His work as 
an educator was marked by thoroughness in every detail. Am- 
bitious for the best for his pupils, he kept in touch with new 
line mind training, greatly enjoying the companionship of his 
fellow teachers. From his school have gone forth many teachers 
whose great aim was to be like their instructor. In discipline 
Mr. Holmes always sought to reach the heart of the wayward 
pupil. On one occasion, when he kept in a girl for her mis- 
demeanor, he was silent for sometime, thinking what would be 
the best way to speak to her. The girl was so impressed by the 
expression of his face and by his silence that she began to 
weep. He won all hearts by his love, his gentleness, his humil-
-P-A-G-E-31
—30— 
ity. His pupils went to him for sympathy in their sorrow, and 
they were equally sure that he would rejoice with them in their 
joys. 
It was a blessing to be daily under the influence of such a re- 
fined, intelligent, well-rounded Christian gentleman, who felt an 
anxious concern for the souls of his pupils. Such a noble char- 
acter must needs make a lasting impression for good on the 
youthful minds, hence it is not unusual to hear his pupils say, 
“ T owe what I am to Mr. Holmes.” 
On September 7, 1894, he heard God’s call, and without a 
moment’s sickness. “He was not: for God took him.”

JOHN H. BRUNNER. 
John H. Brunner was born in Greene County, March 12, 
1825. He attended irregularly the short-term schools of that day 
until 1840, and in the fall of that year and of the next he boarded 
out at better schools. The next year, when 17 years of age, he 
taught two short-term schools. In 1843 he entered Tusculum . 
Academy—soon after made a college—and graduated in 1847, 
Soon after his graduation he joined the Holston Conference, and 
served on appointments for the succeeding five years. In 1850 
he married Elizabeth Key, only sister of David M. Key. He was 
elected Professor in Hiwassee College in 1853. The next year he 
was made its President and served until 1860, when he assumed 
charge of Strawberry Plains College. The Confederate war de- 
stroyed the college, the students left and the building was torn 
down. In 1862 and 1863 he served pastoral charges. In 1864 
he taught some 90 students at Croton Academy until the build- 
ing was burned by incendiaries. He then got a passport to his 
brother’s in Tllinois, where he taught until it was safe to return 
to Tennessee. In 1865 he was reelected President of Hiwassee 
College. Leaving it in competent hands he took charge of the 
Athens Station, and the following year, 1867, was appointed Pre- 
siding Elder of the Athens district. In 1868 he was transferred 
to a Kentucky conference and stationed at Nicholasville. For 
four years, beginning in 1869, he taught in Cane Creek Academy 
—a Peabody school—and enrolled 356 students. In 1873 he was 
again reelected President of Hiwassee College. He was elected 
a delegate to the General Conference of 1877-8, and in the latter 
year was made a Doctor of Divinity. 
In 1879 Sweetwater Institute (Victoria College) became a part 
of Hiwassee College under his care, and the next year Cane 
Creek Academy was added. The enrollment at this time was—

WILLIAM J. GRANNIS. 
When the educational history of Tennessee is fully and fairly 
written one of the brightest and noblest chapters in it will recite 
the story of the life and labors of Prof. William John Grannis, 
A.M., who died at his home in Lebanon, June 19, 1905, at the 
advanced age of 82 years. In the educational development of 
Tennessee he was a pioneer, having come here from his birth- 
place, near Watertown, N. Y., in 1852, in which year he became 
principal of the preparatory department of Cumberland Uni- 
versity. This position he held until 1861, when the troublous 
times of war disturbed all educational conditions, Being a 
Union man, though never an offensive sectionalist, Prof. Gran- 
nis spent the four years of the war in Nashville, where he was 
employed in the quartermaster’s department—1862-1866. In_ 
the latter year he was appointed deputy internal revenue col- 
lector, resigning in 1878 to return to his post at the head of the 
preparatory department of Cumberland University, holding 
this office until, in accordance with the rules of the Southern 
Association of Schools and Colleges, the university determined 
to abolish its preparatory school, which was done in 1902. 
Although in his 80th year, the teaching habit had become so 
fixed in him that he began teaching at once a small select school, 
his chief joy being the pleasure of educating ambitious young 
people, hundreds, aye, thousands of whom, all over the United 
States, gratefully remember him as both their teacher and char- 
acter-builder. A farmer boy till he was 18 years old, he began 
that year his life-work as a teacher, taking a school at Chaumont, 
N. Y., subsequently seeking further preparation in the Normal 
School in Albany, N. Y., where he was graduated in 1848. The 
following four years he taught as principal of the graded schools 
of Cape Vincent, N. Y. 
He always stood among the first of the real educators of Ten- 
nessee. For two years he was President of the State Teachers’ 
Association, and for a generation no State institute was regarded 
as quite complete without him. Though for much of his life 
not actually engaged in public school work, the public schools

teach teachers, and he eagerly accepted all the invitations he 
could, no matter whether he was paid for it or not, if there were

nant traits of character, and whether it was his duty in the 
school room, or as an officer in the Cumberland Presbyterian 
Church, or as a citizen—everywhere it was the same, he was 
there every time and he was there on time. Three children, 
Prof. Herbert W. Grannis, of Castle Heights School, Lebanon,

Mrs. Landrith, wife of Dr. Ira Landrith, of Nashville, survive 
him, his wife having preceded him to heaven ten years ago, her 
death, like his own, being the result of paralysis.

JAMES BYARS. 
James Byars was born in Hanover County, Virginia, in 1819. 
His father, grandfather and great-grandfather had all been prom- 
inent teachers. He was educated for the bar, but decided to de- 
vote his life to teaching. In 1847 he established in Covington, 
Tenn., the Tipton Male High School, and continued as teacher 
and owner of this school until his death in 1901. 
The school was taught in a building erected in his own yard, 
and is still standing. The picture of the building in its present 
condition accompanies this sketch. Many of the most promi- 
nent citizens of West Tennessee were pupils of Prof. Byars in this 
school. Leading citizens in the great cities attribute their suc- 
cess to the instruction received in Tipton Male Academy. 
The following is the only form of advertising used by Mr. 
Byars. It was inserted in his county paper : 
TIPTON MALE ACADEMY. 
Covington, Tipton County, Tenn. 
James Byars, Principal. 
Tuition per session of twenty weeks, payable in advance, $20. 
No deduction made for absence except absence of the teacher. 
Transient scholars, $10 per month. 
Dissipated students—i. e., boys who are in the habit of drinking liquor 
—are requested not to apply for admission into the school. 
The tuition was required to be paid in gold. 
His theory of education represented in its final completeness 
the power of a strong analytical mind to solve what to his gen- 
eration seemed almost insolvable problems. His active mind 
was continuously engaged with education not only as a practice, 
a habit, a mode, an evolution, but as an essential truth, a prin- 
ciple, to ignore which is to defeat the purposes for which the 
human mind operates and human life exists. 
Prof. Byars was the highest type of a true old Virginia gen- 
tleman—honest, cultured, scholarly and true to every trust. He 
deserves to rank as one of the truly great educators of the South.

STATE INSTITUTE, OBION, 
June 5 to July 1, 1905. 
Sir—The people of the good town of Obion were a unit in 
their endeavors to do all possible for the success of the insti- 
tute and for the convenience, comfort, entertainment and pleas- 
ure of the members of the institute and faculty. I have never 
known a town where an institute was located to strive so hard 
or succeed so well in royally entertaining it. They cared for, 
and through their well chosen committees, handled the nearly 
400 teachers who were there with ease, system and success. 
The institute was largely attended. Indeed, it was too large 
for the best results. Of the nearly 400 teachers who enrolled, 
many of them were young, too young to make successful teach- 
ers, and in some instances, to comprehend the work. While this 
is true, we had many mature, well trained and experienced 
teachers who did excellent work, and who will have an influence 
for good in the schools of the State. 
The faculty selected by you were able and efficient workers, 
and were in thorough accord with the conductor in an endeavor 
to give rigid examinations, and require a higher standard for 
State certificates. Much is due them for the success of the in- 
stitute. We desire to thank you for sending to our institute 
Superintendent P. L. Harned and Miss Mattie Butler. Super 
intendent Harned’s lectures were practical and helpful. Miss 
Butler did excellent work for the primary teachers. 
The institute thoroughly enjoyed the addresses delivered by 
Senator Carmack, Gen. H. H. Hannah, Congressmen F. J. Gar- 
rett and the Hon. Joseph E. Washington. 
My experience in this and other institutes leads me to make 
the following recommendations: 
First—That if possible there should be more uniformity in 
both the examination questions and in the grading of papers 
for the State institutes.
